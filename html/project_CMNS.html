Research Topic and Objectives:

Research Topic and Objectives: We clearly defined our research topic as exploring how people's emotional changes during the pandemic influence their consumption habits. Our aim was to conduct the literature search from three main perspectives: changes in people's consumption during the pandemic, the changing mood of people during the pandemic, and broader literature on emotionally influenced consumption.



Empirical Research Studies:

We found three empirical research studies, one focusing on panic buying behavior during the pandemic, and the other two examining changes in dietary and consumption habits during the pandemic. These studies employed empirical methods to investigate the influence of emotional changes on consumption behaviors. While some results supported our hypotheses, others contradicted them.



Non-Empirical Studies:

We also discovered several non-empirical studies that explored the impact of psychological factors on food purchasing decisions during the pandemic. These studies provided theoretical foundations for our research.

Other Sources of Information:

Apart from empirical and non-empirical studies, we gathered information from data tables, bar charts, and news sources. These sources offer valuable insights and support for our research hypotheses.



Conclusion:

After analyzing the literature, we concluded that emotional changes during the pandemic indeed influenced people's consumption habits. However, there were discrepancies in some research findings, and we identified certain limitations and further research opportunities. We proposed the need for in-depth investigations into the long-term effects of emotional changes on consumption habits and the influence of regional factors on consumer behaviors during and after the pandemic.

Final Report

In this research, we aimed to explore the main reasons for mindless consumption behavior during the COVID-19 pandemic and its relationship with negative mentalities triggered by the public health emergency. We conducted mixed qualitative and quantitative research to gain a comprehensive understanding of this phenomenon. The qualitative component involved a discourse analysis of answers to the question "Why did people start to panic buy during the COVID-19 pandemic?" on Quora. We categorized the responses into fear of the unknown and social psychosocial factors, with fear of the unknown (negative mentality) being mentioned most frequently as the primary reason for irrational purchasing. The spread of social misinformation and rumors through news media played a role in influencing the public's consumption behavior, creating a snowball effect. To test the hypothesis that negative mentality increases irrational consumer behavior, we collected quantitative data from Bentall et al.'s study in the UK & Ireland and Hu et al.'s study in China. We used online surveys with questionnaires focused on changes in personal consumption profiles as the dependent variable and negative mentality scales as the independent variable. The results showed that negative mentality significantly positively predicted irrational consumption during the pandemic in both China and the UK & Ireland, with China showing stronger intensity of negative emotions towards mindless consumption. Overall, our findings suggest that negative attitudes generated by a public health emergency are the main drivers of irrational consumption behaviors, such as impulse shopping and overshopping. These attitudes may also be influenced by national epidemic prevention policies or mediated by negative news. This research provides valuable insights into understanding consumer behavior during crises and emphasizes the importance of addressing negative mentalities in managing consumption patterns.
